A Brief History:

Darkroom photography is a process of handling and printing film or negatives (light-sensitive materials) in a room with less light or no light at all.

In the early days of photographic processing, the simplest form was using a darkened room to develop the very first kind of processes, silvered copper plates and papers. The actual term "darkroom" wasn't established until around 1841, when new light sensitive processes were introduced and where such a working space was preferable amonst professionals and enthusiasts.
As the years went on, different processes that developed introduced new types of darkrooms; for instance, the wet-plate process required for images to be developed immediately and as a result make shift darkroom tents were on hand, conversely, the dry plate process allowed images to developed at leisure resulting in a permanent darkroom.
With the dry plate process, gave way to hand held cameras and roll film. The ease provided by these inventions enabled enthusiasts to set up their own darkrooms to process their own films and make their own black and white prints.

Due to the availability of processing equipment, hobbyists of all kinds, both amateurs and professionals, have been able build their own darkrooms.
